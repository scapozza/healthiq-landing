Metabolism

CYP1A2 — Why coffee hits you differently

The CYP1A2 gene controls how quickly your liver breaks down caffeine. Fast metabolizers clear caffeine efficiently and may even see cardiovascular benefits from moderate coffee intake. Slow metabolizers are more susceptible to anxiety, disrupted sleep, and elevated blood pressure.

Kyronix tells you your exact CYP1A2 genotype and gives you practical guidance: when to cut off caffeine, how much is likely optimal, and what that afternoon espresso is probably doing to your HRV and sleep staging.

Nutrient Absorption

Why you might be supplementing wrong

MTHFR affects folate. VDR affects vitamin D. FADS1 affects omega-3 conversion. These aren't obscure edge cases — they're among the most common gene variants and they fundamentally change which supplements you need, in what form, and at what dose.

Kyronix scans your raw DNA file for all major nutrient metabolism variants and builds a supplement protocol based on what your body can actually absorb and use — not what worked for someone else.

MTHFR VDR FADS1 FUT2 (B12) GC (Vitamin D)

MTHFR — Folate Form

Impaired variants need methylfolate, not folic acid. Standard supplements may be largely ineffective for you.

☀️

VDR — Vitamin D Sensitivity

VDR variants affect receptor sensitivity. Some people need higher D3 doses to achieve the same serum levels. Your labs confirm this.

FADS1 — Omega-3 Conversion

Reduced FADS1 activity means ALA from plants converts poorly to EPA/DHA. Pre-formed omega-3s become essential.

Pharmacogenomics

Your DNA affects how your medications work — know your profile

Pharmacogenomics is the science of how your genes affect drug metabolism. CYP2D6, CYP2C19, CYP2C9, and other enzymes process a large proportion of commonly prescribed medications — and your genotype determines whether you're a poor, intermediate, normal, or rapid metabolizer.

Kyronix surfaces your pharmacogenomic profile as a critical safety layer — flagging variants that may make standard doses ineffective or potentially harmful, so you can have informed conversations with your prescribing physician.

CYP2D6 CYP2C19 CYP2C9 CYP3A4
💊

CYP2D6 — The Big One

Metabolizes 25% of all drugs including codeine, SSRIs, beta-blockers, and antipsychotics. 7–10% of people are poor metabolizers.

🫀

CYP2C19 — Clopidogrel & PPIs

Poor metabolizers may see reduced efficacy of Plavix. Relevant for anyone with cardiovascular prescriptions.

🩸

CYP2C9 — Warfarin & NSAIDs

Poor metabolizers require significantly lower warfarin doses. This is one of the clearest clinical pharmacogenomics applications.
