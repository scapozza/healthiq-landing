Metabolizer Types

Poor. Intermediate. Normal. Rapid. Which are you?

Drug metabolism genes classify people into four metabolizer phenotypes. Your phenotype determines whether a standard dose will be too much, too little, or just right — and for some medications, getting it wrong has serious consequences.

Kyronix identifies your metabolizer type for the major drug-metabolizing enzymes from your DNA file and flags which of your medications may be affected — prompting informed conversations with your prescribing physician.

CYP2D6 CYP2C19 CYP2C9 CYP3A4/5
Poor Metabolizer
Drug accumulates. Standard dose may cause toxicity or side effects. Reduced dose often needed.
Intermediate
Reduced metabolism. May respond differently than expected. Dose adjustment may be appropriate.
Normal Metabolizer
Standard dosing generally applies. Expected drug response and clearance rate.
Rapid / Ultrarapid
Drug cleared too quickly. Standard dose may be ineffective. Higher doses may be needed — or alternative drugs.

CYP2D6 — The Most Important One

Metabolizes SSRIs, tricyclics, codeine, tramadol, beta-blockers, antipsychotics, and more. 7–10% of people are poor metabolizers.

🫀

CYP2C19 — Cardiovascular & GI

Metabolizes clopidogrel (Plavix), PPIs (omeprazole), citalopram, and diazepam. Poor metabolizers may have reduced antiplatelet response.

🩸

CYP2C9 — Warfarin & NSAIDs

Poor metabolizers need dramatically lower warfarin doses — one of the clearest clinical pharmacogenomics applications today.

Key Drug Metabolism Genes

CYP2D6, CYP2C19, CYP2C9 — the enzymes that process most of your medications

These three cytochrome P450 enzymes collectively metabolize a large proportion of commonly prescribed drugs. Your genotype for each one is fixed — it doesn't change with age or disease — which is exactly why knowing it early matters so much.

Kyronix maps your CYP genotypes to your medication list and highlights any pharmacogenomic interactions worth discussing with your prescriber or pharmacist.

Drug-Induced Nutrient Depletion

Your medications may be depleting nutrients you don't know you're missing

Many medications deplete essential nutrients as a side effect of their mechanism. Statins block CoQ10 synthesis. Metformin depletes B12. PPIs reduce magnesium, B12, and calcium absorption. Most doctors don't mention this — and most patients don't know.

Kyronix maps your medication list against known drug-nutrient depletion pathways and cross-references with your lab values — then recommends targeted monitoring and supplementation to compensate.

💊

Statins → CoQ10 Depletion

Statins block HMG-CoA reductase — the same pathway that makes CoQ10. Supplementation may help with muscle symptoms.

🫁

PPIs → Magnesium & B12

Long-term PPI use reduces both magnesium absorption and intrinsic factor production, reducing B12 uptake.

🩺

Metformin → B12

Metformin reduces B12 absorption by up to 30%. Annual B12 monitoring is recommended for long-term users.

🌿

St. John's Wort — Wide Interactions

Potent CYP3A4 inducer. Reduces levels of hormonal contraceptives, warfarin, cyclosporine, antiretrovirals, and more.

🐟

Fish Oil — Blood Thinning

High-dose omega-3s may potentiate anticoagulant effects of warfarin and aspirin. Important with cardiovascular medications.

🧄

Grapefruit — CYP3A4 Inhibitor

Grapefruit and grapefruit juice inhibit CYP3A4, dramatically increasing levels of many medications including statins and calcium channel blockers.

Drug–Supplement Interactions

Supplements aren't automatically safe with medications

Supplements are widely perceived as safe because they're "natural" — but they interact with medications through the same liver enzyme pathways as drugs. St. John's Wort, fish oil, vitamin K, and even grapefruit can meaningfully alter drug levels.

Kyronix checks your supplement stack against your medication list and your CYP genotypes to surface interactions you should discuss with your physician or pharmacist — before they become a problem.
